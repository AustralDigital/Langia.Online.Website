Bilingual teams need more than a shared vocabulary list. They need common expectations for meetings, written updates, client communication, and internal collaboration.

## Identify the communication moments

Start by mapping where language friction appears. Teams often need support with presentations, cross-border calls, onboarding, documentation, and customer-facing conversations.

## Build a shared learning rhythm

Short, consistent practice tends to work better than occasional intensive sessions. A weekly cadence helps learners apply new language in real work contexts.

## Measure confidence and clarity

Progress can be tracked through participation, task completion, manager feedback, and learner self-assessment. The goal is clearer communication, not only course attendance.
